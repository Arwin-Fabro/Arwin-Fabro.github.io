About Page

All Earth's water, liquid fresh water, and water in lakes and rivers

All water on Earth in a sphere, placed over a "dry" globe The Earth is a watery place. But just how much water exists on, in, and above our planet? About 71 percent of the Earth's surface is water-covered, and the oceans hold about 96.5 percent of all Earth's water. Water also exists in the air as water vapor, in rivers and lakes, in icecaps and glaciers, in the ground as soil moisture and in aquifers, and even in you and your dog. Water is never sitting still. Thanks to the water cycle, our planet's water supply is constantly moving from one place to another and from one form to another. Things would get pretty stale without the water cycle!

The Major Oceans of The World


There are five oceans on Earth: the Pacific, Atlantic, Indian, Southern (or Antarctic), and Arctic. Pacific Ocean: The largest and deepest ocean, bordering Asia, Australia, and the Americas. Atlantic Ocean: Separates the Americas from Europe and Africa. Indian Ocean: Located primarily between Africa, Asia, and Australia. Southern Ocean: Surrounds Antarctica and is sometimes referred to as the Antarctic Ocean. Arctic Ocean: The smallest and shallowest ocean, located around the North Pole

Ocean Layers

  • Sunlight Zone

    Depth: 0–200 meters
    Light: Full sunlight
    Temperature: Warmest layer
    Creatures: Dolphins, sea turtles, coral reefs, clownfish
    Fun Fact: This is the only ocean zone where plants like seaweed and phytoplankton can grow — it's the ocean’s “rainforest” due to its high biodiversity!

  • Twilight Zone

    Depth: 200–1,000 meters
    Light: Very dim — no sunlight reaches the bottom
    Temperature: Much colder than above
    Creatures: Lanternfish, hatchetfish, glowing jellyfish
    Fun Fact: Many animals here produce their own light using bioluminescence to hunt or communicate.

  • Midnight Zone

    Depth: 1,000–4,000 meters
    Light: Total darkness
    Pressure: Hundreds of times stronger than at sea level
    Creatures: Anglerfish, gulper eel, giant squid
    Fun Fact: Even though it's pitch black, many predators here use glowing lures to attract their prey!

  • Abyssal Zone

    Depth: 4,000–6,000 meters
    Light: Absolute darkness
    Temperature: Near freezing
    Pressure: Bone-crushing
    Creatures: Deep-sea jellyfish, sea cucumbers, basket stars
    Fun Fact: Despite harsh conditions, life thrives here — some animals even glow or have no eyes at all!

  • Hadal Zone

    Depth: 6,000–11,000+ meters
    Light: No light ever
    Location: Found in deep-sea trenches like the Mariana Trench
    Creatures: Snailfish, amphipods, sea cucumbers
    Fun Fact: The pressure here is over 1,000 times stronger than the surface — yet life still exists, including newly discovered species!

Marine Animals

Sperm Whale

Press Here to hear Sperm Whale Sounds

Sperm whales are the largest toothed predators and can dive to great depths in search of squid. They have a distinctive block-shaped head and can hold their breath for over an hour. Sperm whales are known for their deep dives, reaching depths of over 2,000 meters (6,600 feet) to hunt for squid and other prey. They have a unique social structure, often living in matriarchal groups They can produce clicks and whistles for communication and echolocation and can produce sounds up to 230 decibels (dB), making them one of the loudest animals on Earth.

Dolphins

Press Here to hear Dolphins Whistle

Dolphins are highly intelligent marine mammals known for their playful behavior and social nature. They communicate using a variety of clicks, whistles, and body language. Dolphins are known for their acrobatic displays, often leaping out of the water and riding waves. They have a complex social structure, often living in pods of 10 to 30 individuals. Dolphins are also known for their intelligence and problem-solving abilities, often using tools like sponges to protect their snouts while foraging on the seafloor.

Clownfish

Clownfish are small, colorful fish that live in sea anemones. They have a symbiotic relationship with the anemones, which provide them protection from predators. Clownfish are known for their bright orange color with white stripes and can be found in warm waters of the Pacific and Indian Oceans. They are also famous for their role in the animated movie "Finding Nemo." Clownfish are also known for their unique ability to change

Sea Turtle

Sea turtles are large, air-breathing reptiles that live in the ocean. migrations and can live for many decades. Sea turtles are known for their long migrations, often traveling thousands of miles between feeding and nesting grounds. They can hold their breath for several hours while diving and can reach speeds of up to 35 mph (56 km/h) when swimming.

Giant Squid

The giant squid (Architeuthis dux) is a species of deep-ocean dwelling squid in the family Architeuthidae. It can grow to a tremendous size, offering an example of abyssal gigantism: recent estimates put the maximum body size at around 5 m (16 ft) for females, with males slightly shorter, from the posterior fins to the tip of its long arms

Anglerfish

Anglerfish are deep-sea fish known for their bioluminescent lure used to attract prey. They have a unique appearance with a large mouth and sharp teeth.

Coral Reefs

What are Coral Reefs?

Coral reefs are underwater structures made of calcium carbonate, formed by coral polyps. They provide habitat for a diverse range of marine life and are often referred to as the "rainforests of the sea." Coral reefs are vital ecosystems that support a wide variety of marine species, including fish, invertebrates, and algae. They also provide important services such as coastal protection and tourism opportunities.
